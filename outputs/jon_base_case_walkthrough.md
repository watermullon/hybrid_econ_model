# Jon Base Case - Presenter Notes

**The deal:** Deal 1, Southwest US. 116-unit Class C multifamily, value-add.

**The structure:** LP puts in $10M. Fund holds real estate equity, a hedge fund sleeve, and a reserve. LP gets paid out at 2.0x ($20M) before GP takes anything.

**The goal of this walkthrough:** show exactly how $10M becomes $20M over 9 years, where the money sits at each stage, and why the structure is designed the way it is.

---

## Year 0 - The starting position

- **Purchase price: $23M.** LP contributes $10M equity. The deal was structured so the fund opens at $11M NAV. The LP paid $10M for $11M of value on day one. That $1M entry cushion is structural protection built into the deal terms.
- Capital is immediately deployed: $4.5M into RE equity, $5.2M into the hedge fund, $1.3M held in reserve.
- The reserve exists for a reason. It is the buffer for the renovation period ahead when the property will not generate positive cashflow.
- LP hurdle: **$20M cash** (2.0x). Everything in the model is designed around hitting that number.

---

## Year 1 - Renovation underway. The J-curve begins.

- The property is generating $1.28M of NOI but this is a heavy renovation year. Capex of $551k on top of $1.2M debt service produces a net cashflow of **-$473k**. The property is costing more than it earns.
- This is expected and planned for. The reserve absorbs the shortfall. Reserve falls from $1.3M to $827k.
- Meanwhile the hedge fund is quietly compounding at 13%. It grows from $5.2M to $5.9M while the property is being renovated.
- **No LP distributions this year.** The LP is patient.
- Fund NAV: $11.2M. Stable despite the cashflow drag because RE equity and HF are both appreciating.

---

## Year 2 - Still in the J-curve, but improving.

- Renovation continues. NOI grows to $1.57M as occupancy improves, but capex is still $463k. Net cashflow: **-$94k**. Nearly breakeven, but not yet positive.
- Reserve absorbs again, falling to $733k. It is doing its job but getting thinner.
- Property value is ticking up. 3% growth on a $23.7M asset produces $690k of gross appreciation, which translates to a larger equity gain because of the leverage.
- HF grows to $6.6M. The compounding is accumulating quietly in the background.
- **Key point for a viewer:** two years in, the LP has not received a cent. But fund NAV has grown from $11M to $12.6M. The value is being created - it just has not been liquidated yet.

---

## Year 3 - The corner is turned. First cashflow.

- NOI reaches $1.86M, capex drops to $324k as the heavy renovation work winds down. Net RE cashflow: **+$335k**. The first positive year.
- This triggers the **60/25/15 cashflow routing**: 60% to LP distributions ($201k), 25% reinvested into the HF ($84k), 15% to reserve ($50k).
- LP receives its **first distribution: $201k**. Small in absolute terms, but it marks the transition out of the J-curve.
- The 25% reinvestment into the HF is deliberate. Rather than distributing everything now, the model compounds that capital inside the HF where it earns 13% and builds toward the eventual LP payoff.
- Fund NAV: $14.3M.

---

## Year 4 - Stabilisation achieved. First refinance.

- The property hits its **stabilised NOI of $2.15M** - the underwriting target. Capex drops sharply to $236k (now just routine maintenance at 4% of NOI). Net cashflow nearly doubles to **$714k**.
- **Refinance fires** on schedule (Year 4 target). Property is now worth $25.1M. At 70% LTV that supports $17.6M of debt against the $16M existing loan. After 2.5% refi costs, **$1.55M lands as retained cash**.
- This cash is not distributed. It is held deliberately as dry powder for the eventual LP payoff trigger. The refi liability is recorded on the balance sheet.
- LP gets $428k from routing this year. **$629k cumulative** - a long way from $20M, but the property is now generating real cashflow every year.
- **Key point:** the refi extracts equity without selling the asset. The property keeps compounding in value while the cash sits ready for the LP.

---

## Year 5 - Full run rate. HF crosses $10M.

- Everything is running at full speed. NOI $2.21M, capex just $89k, net cashflow **$926k**. The best RE year so far.
- 60/25/15 routing on $926k: $556k to LP, $231k into HF, $139k to reserve.
- **HF crosses $10M for the first time.** It has been compounding at 13% for 5 years, plus receiving annual reinvestments from RE cashflow. This is the engine that will ultimately pay the LP out.
- Fund NAV: $18.5M. Approaching the $20M LP hurdle on paper, but LP has only received **$1.18M in cash**. The fund is healthy, the LP is not yet paid.
- This gap between paper NAV and cash distributions is the central tension. The model is designed to resolve it.

---

## Year 6 - NAV exceeds hurdle. Trigger fires. Fails.

- Fund NAV hits **$21M**. For the first time it exceeds the $20M LP hurdle on paper. The hurdle completion trigger fires automatically.
- The trigger asks: can we assemble enough **cash** to pay the LP the remaining $18.2M right now?
  - HF liquidation (75% cap): $8.8M
  - Retained cash (refi dry powder): $1.6M
  - Reserve: $1.2M
  - **Total: $11.5M. Shortfall: $6.7M.**
- Not enough. The trigger fails and the model moves on. It will retry next year.
- The 75% liquidation cap is a deliberate constraint. We do not want to liquidate the entire HF to pay the LP because that destroys the GP's remaining upside.
- LP gets another $594k from routing. The drip of distributions continues.
- **Key point:** the fund is economically solvent. The LP will get paid - it is a question of when, not if.

---

## Year 7 - Second refinance. Trigger fails again, but barely.

- **Second refinance fires** (scheduled Year 7 target). Property now worth $27.5M. Same 70% LTV mechanic produces **$1.6M net proceeds** added to retained cash.
- Retained cash is now **$3.2M** - two refi tranches stacked and waiting.
- Trigger fires again with the larger cash pool: HF $10.1M + retained $3.2M + reserve $1.3M = **$14.6M**. LP needs $17.6M. **Shortfall: $3.0M.**
- The gap has closed from $6.7M to $3.0M in one year. The HF compounding is doing the work.
- **Key point:** the refi strategy is working. Each year the HF gets larger and the retained cash grows. The trigger will succeed soon.

---

## Year 8 - One year away.

- HF grows to **$15.5M**. The single largest asset in the fund, now larger than the RE equity.
- Trigger: HF $11.6M + retained $3.2M + reserve $1.5M = **$16.3M** vs $16.9M needed. **Shortfall: just $0.6M.**
- One more year of 13% compounding on a $15.5M HF base adds over $2M. The crossing point is next year.
- LP distributions continue from routing: $674k this year, $3.1M cumulative.

---

## Year 9 - LP paid out. Fund closes.

- The HF has grown to $17.5M opening balance (13% on $15.5M). The trigger fires and succeeds.
- **How the LP gets paid $16.9M this year:**
  - HF liquidated (75% cap): $11.3M
  - Retained cash (two refi tranches): $3.2M
  - Reserve: $1.7M
  - RE cashflow routing (this year): $716k
  - **Total: $16.9M**
- Combined with $3.1M received in prior years: **LP cash total = $20M. 2.0x achieved.**
- **GP residual NAV: $13.9M**
  - RE net equity $10.6M, minus refi liability $3.2M, plus HF residual $6.5M (the 25% not liquidated)
- The GP keeps the remaining HF position and the property. Neither was sold.

---

## The headline

| | |
|---|---|
| LP invested | $10M |
| LP returned | $20M (2.0x cash MOIC) |
| Hold period | 9 years |
| GP residual NAV | $13.9M |

**The structure in one sentence:** a stabilising real estate asset funds a compounding hedge fund; the hedge fund does the heavy lifting on the LP payoff; two scheduled refinances build the cash reserve that closes the gap; the property is never sold.

**Why this beats a straight RE deal:** a conventional deal distributes cashflow annually and relies on a sale to return capital. Here, cashflow is reinvested into the HF at 13%, compounding far faster than a property can appreciate. The LP gets 2x without a sale. The GP retains the asset and keeps the residual upside.
